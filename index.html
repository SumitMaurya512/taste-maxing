The Taste-Skill Gap

This gap is normal. It's what got you into creative work.

You can recognise brilliant work. You know when something is extraordinary. You feel it in your body — that pang of "this is good" when you see a perfectly graded film, a typeface used exactly right, a building that makes you stop.

But when you sit down to make something, it falls short. Your hands haven't caught up with your eyes.

This is not imposter syndrome. It's the natural state of anyone whose perception outpaces their craft.

The only way to close the gap: volume. Make a huge amount of work. Put yourself on a deadline. Do it every day.

EXERCISE

The Gap Test

Pick 3 pieces of work you love — from any field. Design, film, architecture, food, music. Pull them up on your phone or write them down. Now: try to explain exactly why each one is good. Not "it feels right." Specifics.

  • "I like the colour palette" is not enough — which colours? Why those together?
  • "The composition is nice" is banned — what's in the foreground? Where does your eye go?
  • When you struggle to explain — you've found the gap.
5:00 TAP TO START

The gap between your taste and your ability is the engine.

Don't run from it. Use it.

The Workshop Contradiction
Position A
John Berger

"Taste is social conditioning, not personal development. You see what your culture trained you to see."

VS
Position B
Goodspeed + Housen

"Taste is absolutely learnable and trainable. It develops in measurable stages, like any skill."

Both are true. You are shaped by what surrounds you — which means you can deliberately shape your surroundings. That's the entire premise of this workshop.
EXERCISE

Articulation
Challenge

I'll show you two pieces of work. Write down exactly why one is better than the other. "It feels right" is banned. You must use specific observations — colour, composition, weight, rhythm, negative space, contrast.

  • Write at least 3 sentences for each piece
  • Name the specific element that creates the difference
  • If you can't articulate it — you've found your next growth edge

EXERCISE

The 2-Minute
Look

I'll play 2 minutes of a film. Watch it normally. Write what you noticed.

Then I'll teach you how to look. We'll watch the same clip again. Write what you notice now.

  • First pass: gut reaction, general impression
  • After instruction: light direction, lens choice, blocking, colour grading, sound design
  • Compare your two lists — the delta is the skill you just gained

Chapter Five

DAILY
PRACTICE

Taste compounds like interest. But only if you practice. Six habits, six frequencies.

Habits & Systems

Practices that compound

Morning Pages
juliacameronlive.com

3 pages, longhand, stream of consciousness, every morning. 12 weeks reveals actual taste vs. stated taste.

3 pages, longhand, stream of consciousness, every morning. From Julia Cameron's The Artist's Way. 12 weeks of morning pages reveals your actual taste vs. your stated taste. The practice works because it bypasses your inner critic — you write before the editorial voice wakes up. Most people discover they care about completely different things than they thought.

Creative Ritual Daily / 20min
The Logbook
austinkleon.com

One page of daily lists: what you looked at, read, watched, noticed. Patterns over weeks reveal actual aesthetic interests.

One page of daily lists: what you looked at, read, watched, noticed. Austin Kleon's practice from Steal Like an Artist. Patterns emerge over weeks — you discover your actual aesthetic interests vs. what you think you're interested in. The list format removes the pressure of 'writing well' and lets you just observe.

Observation Daily / 5min
Articulation Test
shaping.design

Pick two pieces. Write exactly why one is better. "It feels right" is banned. Replace with specific observations.

Pick two pieces of work. Write exactly why one is better. 'It feels right' is banned — you must replace it with specific observations about colour, composition, rhythm, weight, negative space. This is the single most powerful exercise for turning unconscious taste into conscious skill. The struggle IS the learning.

Critical Writing Weekly / 15min
Continued

Curation, auditing, archiving

Swipe File
blog.readymag.com

Capture what stops you. Apply Spark-Strength-Service filter weekly. The deletion practice is as important as the capture.

Capture what stops you. Apply the Spark-Strength-Service filter weekly: Does it spark something? Is it technically strong? Can it serve your work? The deletion practice is as important as the capture — ruthlessly remove saves that no longer resonate. Your swipe file is a mirror of your evolving taste.

Reference Ongoing
Diverse-Ratio Audit
itsnicethat.com

Review inspiration sources monthly. Taste that only references contemporary work in your field is a closed loop.

Review your inspiration sources monthly. If 80% of your references are from the same decade, medium, and culture — you're in a closed loop. Break it: add architecture if you only follow graphic design. Add 1970s film if you only watch contemporary work. Taste that references only itself calcifies.

Source Audit Monthly / 30min
Twyla Tharp's Box
Physical Practice

A physical box per project. Everything relevant goes in. What you keep reaching for reveals your actual taste.

Twyla Tharp's method from The Creative Habit: start every project with a physical cardboard box. Everything related goes in — clippings, notes, photos, objects, scraps. When you're stuck, reach into the box. What you keep reaching for reveals your actual taste. What stays at the bottom reveals what you thought mattered but didn't.
